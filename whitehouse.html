The seat of the President

Early residences

Multiple early residences for the president were built. The first of these houses was the Samuel Osgood house, in Manhattan, New York.
It was used as the first presidental house, by George Washington from April 1789 to February 1790, after his famous inauguration.
It was also named after the existing Treasury Commissioner at the time, Samuel Osgood. It was known as the First Presidential Mansion.


The president then moved into a bigger house called the Alexander Macomb House, named after the Irish trader who built the house.
It was located at 39-41 Broadway, and was manned by a small selection of staff. Washington stayed there until August 1790.


Beginning in May 1790, New York started constructing a permanent house for the President, called the Government House.
However, the national capital was moved to Philadelphia in Pennsylvania before the house was constructed, so no president ever lived there.


After the national capital was temporarily moved to Philadelphia, the house of merchant Robert Morris was rented for the President,
calling it the President's House while Washington lived there from November 1790 to March 1797 with 30 staff and servants.


Like New York, Philadelphia also began constructing a house for the President, but the president at the time of completion declined to inhabit it.
It was later sold to the city's university, around the time when the capital was moved to Washington D.C (District of Columbia)


The White House

The building most associated with the president of the USA however, is the iconic White House. Built of white
painted locally sourced Aquia Creek sandstone, it stands at a hefty 5,500m², roughly the size of 2 or 3
supermarkets. It was built by James Hoban, an Irish architect, from 1792 to 1800.
